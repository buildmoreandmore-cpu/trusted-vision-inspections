Why Radon Testing Matters for Atlanta Homebuyers

Radon is an invisible, odorless radioactive gas that seeps into homes through cracks in the foundation, gaps around pipes, and openings in the building envelope. According to the Environmental Protection Agency, radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, responsible for an estimated 21,000 deaths each year. The only way to know if a home has elevated radon levels is to test for it, and that is exactly what Trusted Vision Home Inspections provides for Metro Atlanta homebuyers.

Many people assume that radon is only a concern in northern states with cold climates and basement construction, but that is a dangerous misconception. The EPA has identified portions of Georgia, including several counties in the Metro Atlanta region, as Zone 2 areas with predicted average indoor radon levels between 2 and 4 picocuries per liter (pCi/L). Approximately 19% of homes tested in Georgia have radon levels at or above the EPA action level of 4.0 pCi/L. The granite bedrock underlying much of the northern Metro Atlanta area -- the Piedmont geological province -- naturally produces uranium, which decays into radon gas and migrates upward through the soil into homes. Communities in North Fulton, Cobb, Cherokee, Forsyth, and Gwinnett counties are particularly susceptible.

Even in areas with lower geological risk, individual homes can test high based on their specific construction, soil conditions, and foundation type. A home built on a slab foundation with cracks or poorly sealed joints can allow significant radon entry, while the neighboring home may test below detectable levels. This is why the EPA recommends testing every home regardless of location.

Our 48-Hour Continuous Monitoring Process

Carl Hall uses professional-grade continuous radon monitors (CRMs) that are calibrated and maintained to the highest standards. Unlike passive charcoal test kits that provide only a snapshot average, our continuous monitors record radon levels every hour over a minimum 48-hour testing period. This provides a detailed hourly profile that shows how radon levels fluctuate throughout the day and night, giving you a far more accurate and reliable result that is also tamper-evident.

The testing process is straightforward and minimally intrusive. Carl places the continuous monitor in the lowest livable level of the home, typically on the main floor or in a finished basement. The monitor must remain undisturbed for a minimum of 48 hours with closed-building conditions maintained, meaning windows and exterior doors should be kept closed except for normal entry and exit. After the testing period, Carl retrieves the monitor, downloads the data, and includes the results in your inspection report with a clear explanation of what the numbers mean and whether mitigation is recommended.

What Happens If Radon Levels Are High

If results come back above the EPA action level of 4.0 pCi/L, Carl will explain your options for radon mitigation. A qualified radon mitigation contractor can install a sub-slab depressurization system that typically reduces radon levels by 80 to 99 percent. The cost of mitigation typically ranges from $800 to $2,500, and many buyers successfully negotiate this cost into the home purchase agreement when elevated levels are found during the inspection period. Carl can provide referrals to qualified, licensed radon mitigation contractors in Metro Atlanta.

Radon testing is available as an add-on to any home inspection for just $200, or as part of our Complete Protection Bundle at $899. Radon Testing FAQs

Common questions about radon testing in Metro Atlanta.

Is radon really a problem in Atlanta, Georgia?

Yes. Approximately 19% of homes tested in Georgia have radon levels at or above the EPA action level of 4.0 pCi/L. The northern Metro Atlanta area is particularly at risk due to the underlying granite geology of the Piedmont region. Communities in North Fulton, Cobb, Cherokee, Forsyth, and Gwinnett counties are especially susceptible. Individual homes can test high regardless of zone, which is why testing every home is the only way to know for certain.

How does 48-hour continuous monitoring work?

Carl places a professional-grade continuous radon monitor (CRM) in the lowest livable area of the home. The device records radon levels every hour for a minimum of 48 hours under closed-building conditions. Unlike passive charcoal kits that give you a single average number, our CRM produces an hourly data log that shows exactly how radon levels fluctuate over the testing period. This gives you a much more accurate and reliable result that can also detect tampering or unusual conditions.

What happens if radon levels are high?

If your test results exceed the EPA action level of 4.0 pCi/L, Carl will include a detailed explanation in your report along with recommendations. Radon mitigation is a well-established process that typically involves installing a sub-slab depressurization system. A qualified mitigation contractor can reduce radon levels by 80 to 99 percent, and the cost typically ranges from $800 to $2,500. Many buyers negotiate mitigation costs into the home purchase agreement during the due diligence period.

Do new-construction homes need radon testing?

Absolutely. New homes can have elevated radon levels just like older properties. Radon enters through the soil beneath the foundation, regardless of the home's age. Even homes built with radon-resistant construction features should be tested to verify that the system is working effectively. Georgia building codes do not require radon-resistant construction in all areas, so testing is the only way to confirm your indoor air quality is safe.

Can radon be fixed in an existing home?

Yes. Radon mitigation is highly effective and can reduce indoor radon levels by up to 99%. The most common solution is an active soil depressurization system, which involves installing a pipe and fan system that draws radon gas from beneath the foundation and vents it safely above the roofline. These systems are quiet, energy-efficient, and can be installed in most homes within a single day.
